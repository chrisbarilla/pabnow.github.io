The City of Rochester fails to hold police officers accountable for misconduct and brutality. Between 2001 and 2016, citizens filed 929 allegations of excessive force against the RPD. The Chief of Police, who has the sole power to discipline officers, only sustained 16 of these allegations. This is injustice. And the reason is obvious: the Chief of Police cannot be expected to investigate, evaluate, and discipline police officers. This is a clear conflict of interest. Even in the rare instance that the Chief does discipline an officer, the punishment is inadequate; Between 2002 and 2016, only 13 officers were disciplined, of those only 6 were suspended, and none were fired. Police misconduct will continue unless this broken system is replaced with an independent system that serves the interest of the people of Rochester not the RPD.

A coalition of community organizations came together in 2017 and proposed a Police Accountability Board (PAB) to replace the current broken system. The proposed PAB would be a civilian led oversight board that is rooted in five essential pillars:

  • an independent agency of city government, separate from RPD, with a budget equivalent to 1% of RPD's
  • independent investigative authority
  • subpoena power
  • disciplinary power through a disciplinary matrix
  • power to assess RPD policies and procedures and make recommendations

The majority of the board members of the PAB would be nominated by a coalition of the Rochester community organizations called the Police Accountability Board Alliance. The Alliance removes the conflict of interest and replaces it with a democratic, civilian-lead oversight system.

AJ's Intro

The Problem: The City of Rochester fails to hold police officers accountable for misconduct and brutality.

  • Between 2001 and 2016, citizens filed 923 allegations of excessive force against the RPD.
  • The Chief of Police, who has the sole power to discipline officers, only sustained 16 of these allegations.
  • The Chief of Police cannot be expected to investigate, evaluate, and discipline police officers. This is a clear conflict of interest.
  • Between 2002 and 2016, only 13 officers were disciplined. Of those disciplined, only 6 were suspended. None were fired.
  • For more information and data about the current system and how it has failed, see [link to Ted and Barbara’s report].

Police misconduct will continue unless this broken system is replaced with an independent system that serves the interests of the people of Rochester, not just the RPD.

The Solution: A coalition of community organizations came together in 2017 and proposed a Police Accountability Board (PAB) to replace the current broken system. The proposed PAB would be a civilian led oversight board that is rooted in five essential pillars:

  • an independent agency of city government, separate from RPD
  • The power to independently investigate complaints of police misconduct
  • Subpoena power to compel the production of evidence and witnesses
  • Disciplinary power
  • The power to review and evaluate RPD patterns, practices, policies and procedures to recommend systemic changes and to prevent misconduct from happening in the first place.

In addition to the five pillars, sufficient funding is required so that complaints of misconduct are resolved efficiently and effectively in 90 days. To handle the volume of complaints that are filed in Rochester, the PAB will require a funding equal to 1% of RPD’s budget, one million dollars, a reasonable cost of true accountability, and an investment which will result in far fewer costly lawsuits against the city involving excessive force.

The majority of the board members of the PAB would be nominated by a coalition of the Rochester community organizations called the Police Accountability Board Alliance. The Alliance removes the conflict of interest and replaces it with a civilian-lead oversight system, empowering the communities that have had no direct control over how they are policed for far too long.
